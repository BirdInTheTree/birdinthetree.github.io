Эмпатия к персонажу

Персонажи
obergnashhaugelandaulevy

Суть

Эмпатия — краеугольный камень сторителлинга: если мы не переживаем за персонажей, нам безразлична история [1]. Hauge выделяет пять методов создания эмпатии: незаслуженное несчастье, опасность, likability, юмор и мастерство [2]. Nash расширяет это до восьми черт «rooting resume»: Underdog, Powerful, In Jeopardy, Likable, Funny, Skilled, Wronged, Unique [3] — чем больше черт, тем сильнее притяжение [4]. Oberg подчёркивает, что эмпатия — это не обязательно симпатия: достаточно понимать конфликт персонажа и видеть баланс между надеждой и страхом [5][6]. Victimization — самый надёжный приём для создания сочувствия [8], а vulnerability — источник подлинной человеческой связи [7].

Цитаты

[1] "Empathy is the cornerstone of great storytelling. If we don't care about the characters, we won't care about the story." — Landau, p.20

[2] "There are five basic methods for creating empathy: make the character the victim of some undeserved misfortune; put the character in jeopardy; make the character likeable; make the character funny; make the character powerful or good at what she does." — Hauge, p.33

[3] "The eight traits of the rooting resume: Underdog, Powerful, In Jeopardy, Likable, Funny, Skilled, Wronged, Unique." — Nash, p.28

[4] "You don't need all eight. But the more you have, the stronger the pull." — Nash, p.29

[5] "The pilot has to make us fall in love with the characters. Protagonists, antagonists, returning characters: if we don't want to know what's going to happen to the characters, the series is dead. Again, this doesn't mean we have to like them as in having sympathy for them or approving of their goals. Just that we understand them and can relate to their conflict." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.208

[6] "Strike the right balance between hope and fear. Do we fear that the protagonist might fail because the sources of conflict are strong enough, but also hope that they stand a chance of succeeding? [...] Most of us won't relate to passive victims, because it's alienating." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.209

[7] "Vulnerability is the birthplace of love, belonging, joy, courage, empathy, and creativity. It is the source of hope, empathy, accountability, and authenticity." — Landau, p.145

[8] "Victimization is the most reliable technique for creating sympathy and rooting interest. Audiences instinctively root for underdogs, for people who have been dealt a bad hand or who are struggling against unfair circumstances." — Levy, p.150

[9] "Clint Eastwood once described his approach to getting audiences to root for his characters: 'In the fifth minute of the film, some people will kill his wife and his children and burn his house down.' It's extreme but it makes the point: victimize your hero early." — Levy, p.151

[10] "By the end, the pilot has reached most of the goals that we defined. One of the most important ones being striking the right balance between hope and fear. This is achieved perfectly in Killing Eve because Eve is completely out of her depth [...] So we fear for Eve. At the same time, we have hope because she's clever, resourceful and she has good instincts." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.3, p.225

[11] "Rooting interest is perhaps the single most important criterion network executives use when evaluating pilot scripts. Do we root for the lead character? Do we care what happens to them?" — Levy, p.148

[12] "Every great TV character has a rooting resume — a list of traits that make us want to follow them." — Nash, p.28

[13] "Your hero should be described in terms of setup and empathy. Don't simply say 'a woman' — say 'a single mom who's barely holding her life together.' That single phrase creates both a picture of her setup and empathy for her situation." — Hauge, p.44

[14] "Victimization, surprise and paradox are all good ways to make a character pop in a pitch. Victimization generates sympathy. Surprise creates interest. Paradox generates intrigue." — Levy, p.114

[15] "A good strategy is to describe each character in two steps: First, quickly describe the type -- the archetype. What's the quick handle for the character? Then tell the buyer what makes the character distinct, different or unexpected -- the thing that makes the character unique." — Levy, p.112
