Персонажи

Суть

Ансамбль — это множество параллельных transformation machines, работающих одновременно [1]. Каждый персонаж ансамбля нуждается в собственном flaw, want и need [2]. Поддерживающие персонажи не существуют изолированно: каждый из них связан с flaw героя — разделяет его, отражает, контрастирует или ставит под вопрос [3]. Они же представляют разные ответы на тематический вопрос сериала [4]. Oberg подчёркивает, что character breakdowns должны включать не только описание каждого персонажа, но и его роль в истории [5]. Levy добавляет: описывай персонажей не изолированно, а как сеть отношений — напряжений, союзов, зависимостей и конфликтов [6].

Цитаты

[1] "Ensembles are just multiple transformation machines running at the same time." — Nash, p.86

[2] "Every character in an ensemble needs their own flaw, their own want, their own need. They each need their own transformation machine." — Nash, p.86

[3] "Every supporting character should have a relationship to the hero's flaw. They either share it, reflect it, contrast it, or challenge it." — Nash, p.32

[4] "Supporting characters orbit the hero. Each one represents a different answer to the thematic question." — Nash, p.32

[5] "Character breakdowns should include: the character's name, age, occupation, a brief physical description, their main personality traits, their conscious goal and unconscious need, their main flaw or issue, and their role in the story (protagonist, antagonist, co-protagonist, catalyst)." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Bible Components, p.199

[6] "Build your characters as an interconnected ensemble. Don't describe each character in isolation. Describe the relationships and dynamics between characters -- the tensions, alliances, dependencies and conflicts that will generate stories." — Levy, p.116

[7] "Character design in a series means thinking about: Who is the protagonist? Who is the antagonist (or catalyst in a character-led story)? Are there co-protagonists? What are their conscious goals and unconscious needs? What's the main relationship that drives the story? How do the characters evolve or grow over the season and the series?" — Oberg, Chapter 3, Series Design, p.190

[8] "Don't name more than three characters in your pitch. The more names you drop, the harder it is for the listener to follow. Use descriptions instead: 'the dad,' 'his partner,' 'her best friend.'" — Hauge, p.49

[9] "Your enemy's enemy is your friend." — Landau, p.197

[10] "With multiple protagonists you can either have a single goal that they are all working towards or give each protagonist their own separate goal." — Harris, p.31

[11] "The studio gave an interesting set of notes: King's initial drafts compartmentalized the four girls' stories into separate acts, and the studio said the girls felt too disconnected from each other and from the core ensemble. They said the script played too episodic, rather than establishing a world of interconnected relationships." — Levy, p.263

[12] "Character names starting with different letters. It's a small detail but it makes a big difference. Buyers are hearing a lot of pitches, and if every character name starts with a different letter, it's much easier to keep them straight." — Levy, p.119
