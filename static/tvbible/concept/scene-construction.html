Конструкция сцены

harrislandaunashoberg

Суть

Каждая сцена имеет собственное Scene DNA: герой сцены, цель сцены и препятствие; конфликт — это столкновение препятствия с целью [1]. Обязательное условие сцены — эмоциональное изменение: персонаж входит в сцену в одном состоянии и выходит в другом [2]. Если в сцене нет конфликта и эмоционального изменения — это не сцена, а переход; его нужно вырезать или исправить [3]. Каузальность критична: события должны происходить вследствие предыдущих, а не случайно [4]. Landau добавляет: каждая сцена должна нести собственный конфликт, давать новую информацию о сюжете или персонаже и развлекать [5]. Scène à faire — обязательная сцена, к которой строится весь нарратив; это сцена, которую зритель ждёт [6].

Цитаты

[1] "Scene DNA: every scene needs a scene hero, a scene goal, and a scene obstacle. The conflict is the obstacle pushing against the goal. Mark it with ><." — Nash, p.230

[2] "Every scene must have an emotional change. The character enters the scene feeling one way and leaves feeling another. Mark it with +/- or -/+." — Nash, p.230

[3] "If a scene doesn't have conflict and emotional change, it's not a scene. It's a transition. Cut it or fix it." — Nash, p.231

[4] "Causality is essential in storytelling. Events should happen as a result of what happened before, not by coincidence. If you can take out or swap events without disrupting the story, there isn't enough causality." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Case Study: Stranger Things, p.148

[5] "By and large, every scene needs to have its own conflict, offer up a new piece of information about plot or character, and entertain/intrigue along the way." — Landau, p.221

[6] "The scène à faire – literally 'scene that must be done' – is the obligatory scene that the entire narrative has been building toward. It's the scene the audience has been waiting for." — Landau, p.15

[7] "Skip breakfast. Start your scenes as late as possible. We don't need to see people wake up, shower, and drive to work." — Nash, p.78

[8] "Each card on the board represents a scene. One scene. One card." — Nash, p.225

[9] "Double bump — when you combine two problems into one scene, it has twice the impact. One event affects two stories simultaneously." — Nash, p.105

[10] "Essentially, in doing this I'm creating a log line for each scene - two to three sentences each - using the most evocative language I can to bring them to life." — Harris, p.136

[11] "Mix, match, and collapse: once all your cards are on the board, look for scenes that can be combined. Two scenes in the same location with the same characters? Maybe they're one scene." — Nash, p.234
