Пилотный эпизод

Структура
landaulevynashoberg

Суть

Пилот преследует две главные цели: получить заказ на сериал и зацепить аудиторию [1]. Существуют два основных типа пилотов: premise pilot (показывает начало истории, как Breaking Bad) и non-premise/midstream pilot (погружает в уже идущую историю, как The Office) [2]. В пилоте герой должен быть на пике своего изъяна — в самой флаговой версии себя — и лишь показать «whiff of change», намёк на возможную трансформацию, а не саму трансформацию [3][4]. Пилот — это proof of concept: он должен продемонстрировать всё, чем будет сериал — franchise, тон, персонажей, мир [5]. Финал пилота должен быть достаточно завершённым, чтобы удовлетворить зрителя, но оставить достаточно открытых вопросов для продолжения — стратегия «baby steps» [6]. Конец пилота — это начало сериала, а не его завершение [7].

Цитаты

[1] "A pilot has two main aims. First, it's to set the right creative start for the series, get the series commissioned and then produced. Then, once produced, it's to hook the audience into the series." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.206

[2] "There are two main types of pilots: A premise pilot shows the beginning of the story, before the main action starts or the main concept is introduced. That's how Stranger Things, Sex Education or Breaking Bad begin. A midstream or 3rd episode pilot shows the story already going on, without showing its starting point. That's how The Office begins, both in the original UK series and the U.S. remake." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.211

[3] "In the pilot, you're at the beginning of the transformation map. The character is at their most flawed." — Nash, p.82

[4] "A whiff of change — in the pilot, the hero doesn't fully transform, but we need to see a hint that change is possible. A small moment where they glimpse the better version of themselves." — Nash, p.80

[5] "The pilot is a proof of concept. It needs to show everything the series will be." — Nash, p.79

[6] "The pilot ending strategy I call 'baby steps': resolve enough of the pilot story to satisfy the audience, but don't resolve everything. Leave enough unresolved to compel viewers to tune in next week. Satisfying but not complete." — Levy, p.161

[7] "The pilot episode is the beginning of your series. The end of your pilot is the beginning of your series. Not the end." — Landau, p.87

[8] "The goals for the pilot are to: 1) Get the series commissioned, 2) Hook the audience from the beginning, 3) Keep the audience interested as we introduce the story world and characters, 4) Make sure that by the end of the pilot we care both about the characters and the story, 5) Define the stakes, 6) Show that drama is a struggle, not a statement, 7) Set the right balance between hope and fear, 8) Provide a satisfying ending, 9) Establish a theme relevant to the audience, 10) Demonstrate the series franchise." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.207

[9] "Hook the audience from the beginning so that they want to know what's going to happen next, first in the pilot itself, then in the rest of the series." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.207

[10] "The opening pitch needs to do three things: introduce the tone, introduce the franchise, and hook the audience." — Nash, p.73

[11] "You want to have started the main conflict in the episode — if not of the season or of the series — so that the audience knows what's at stake, why they should keep watching. If we understand 'Who needs or wants what and why?' by minute ten, if we're aware of the main dramatic action or evolution in the episode, through managing conflict, then we'll know why we should keep watching." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.215

[12] "If you don't achieve this by minute fifteen, preferably minute ten, or find a way around that using managing information, your chances of the pilot being pushed back to the bottom of the pile or being tossed in the bin go up exponentially." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.216

[13] "The window character is someone new to the world of the show. They're our way in. They ask the questions we want to ask." — Nash, p.76

[14] "Moving trains — when a pilot starts, things are already happening. The characters have lives in progress. The world is already turning." — Nash, p.75

[15] "A mini-episode is a complete small story at the top of the pilot that demonstrates what the show is about." — Nash, p.74

[16] "In a pilot, the Break into 3 is the Break into Series. It's the moment where the character commits to the world of the show." — Nash, p.122

[17] "Series beats and pilot beats overlap but they're not the same. The pilot's Catalyst might be the series' Catalyst. Or it might not." — Nash, p.123

[18] "Let them know if it's a 'premise' pilot or a 'non-premise' pilot. A premise pilot means that whatever happens in episode #1 is essential to start the series from day one." — Landau, p.316

[19] "One of the most important goals of a pilot is to prove the concept -- to demonstrate what a typical episode of the series will look like." — Levy, p.163

[20] "The Pilot Story section of the pitch is the heart of the pitch. It should take about five to seven minutes to deliver." — Levy, p.121

[21] "End the pilot story with a satisfying resolution that also promises there's more story to tell." — Levy, p.128

[22] "Stick the landing: End your pitch of the pilot episode on a high note that portends future conflicts versus too much resolution and harmony." — Landau, p.317

[23] "The pilot script is the calling card that will determine the creative direction and potential longevity of the series." — Landau, p.3

[24] "If it's in the show, it's in the pilot." — Nash, p.79

[25] "The pilot must establish the rules of the world. If magic exists, show magic. If it's a world without cell phones, show that too." — Nash, p.67

[26] "The guided tour is the part of the pilot where we're shown the world, the characters, and the rules. It's exposition disguised as entertainment." — Nash, p.77

[27] "A premise pilot has a built-in Catalyst. The non-premise pilot has to manufacture one." — Nash, p.71

[28] "Let the games begin! — In a pilot, the Final Image should be the start of the series. It's the image that says: 'This is the show you'll be watching.'" — Nash, p.110

[29] "Every episode, the character steps up to the line of change... and then steps back." — Nash, p.11
