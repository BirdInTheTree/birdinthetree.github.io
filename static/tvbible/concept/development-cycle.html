levy

Суть

Development — это процесс превращения идеи в сценарий, служащий чертежом для пилота или сериала [1]. Процесс включает двенадцать шагов: от идеи через концепцию, питч, outline, пилотный сценарий до решения о запуске серии [2]. Большинство проектов гибнет на этом пути — на broadcast-сетях соотношение сценариев к заказанным пилотам примерно 10:1 («Пирамида смерти») [3]. Дистрибуция телевидения радикально меняется, но сам процесс разработки остаётся фундаментально прежним [4]. Netflix внедрил модель cost-plus и data-driven development, а также straight-to-series заказы, но для этого требуется от полутора лет подготовки [5][6]. Типы сделок варьируются от if-come (работа на spec без гарантий) до overall-deal (студия владеет всеми идеями автора) [7][8]. Bake-off — конкурс авторов за одно open writing assignment — стандартная практика [9].

Цитаты

[1] "Development is the process of originating and improving scripted material to serve as a blueprint for a TV pilot, series or feature film." — Levy, p.11

[2] "The development process can be broken down into twelve steps: 1) Ideation, 2) Concept development, 3) Assess the buyer marketplace, 4) Pitch, 5) Story outline, 6) Pilot script, 7) Notes and revisions, 8) Internal pilot review, 9) Pilot production, 10) Post-production, 11) Network review, 12) Series order (or not)." — Levy, p.28

[3] "Most projects in television development never make it to pilot, though. At the legacy broadcast networks the ratio is about 10:1. Only about 10% of pilot scripts get ordered to production. Graham Yost, creator and Executive Producer of Justified, calls this 'the Pyramid of Death.'" — Levy, p.12

[4] "The distribution of TV is being disrupted, but the development of TV isn't. With some notable exceptions, most TV development is practiced the same way it has been practiced for decades." — Levy, p.10

[5] "Netflix often pays what's known as 'cost-plus' fees, the cost of production plus a built-in, pre-determined 'profit' fee, in exchange for all distribution, worldwide, in perpetuity." — Levy, p.245

[6] "With straight-to-series projects sellers spend much more than a few months assembling a substantial and thorough package of creative elements. The House of Cards team, for example, spent a year and a half developing the project internally before taking it out to network buyers." — Levy, p.241

[7] "In an if-come deal, a studio guarantees the writer a specific amount of money to write the pilot script if a network buys it. In other words, the writer agrees to develop and shop a pitch on spec with no guaranteed payment." — Levy, p.244

[8] "The most significant kind of development deals studios make are 'overall deals,' where the studio pays the writer to own all TV ideas, pitches, pilots and series the writer creates. A step down in level of commitment is a 'first-look deal' in which the studio pays a writer only for a first look at the writer's ideas, a first right of refusal." — Levy, p.253

[9] "A 'bake-off,' one of my favorite expressions in TV development, refers to the competitive filling of an open writing assignment. In a bake-off, at least two writers go into a network and vie for the chance to fill an open writing assignment by pitching their takes on the project." — Levy, p.275

[10] "Pretty Little Liars, which I developed and produced, spent five years from ideation to series premiere, surviving three different networks, three different teams of development execs and three different writers until we got it right." — Levy, p.256

[11] "Netflix uses its data and algorithms to specifically assess if a show about a specific kind of subject matter, set in a specific world with specific actors, will appeal to its audience, and often makes development decisions based on the results of that math." — Levy, p.246

[12] "TV networks typically get two cracks at developing projects once they buy them. These are known as 'bites.'" — Levy, p.258
