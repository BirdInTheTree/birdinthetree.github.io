M-Factor: управление информацией

landaulevyoberg

Суть

M-Factor (management of information) — третье ключевое измерение сценарного мастерства наряду со структурой и типом истории; оно определяет, кто знает что и когда — писатель, персонажи и аудитория [1]. Четыре основных режима: dramatic irony (зритель знает больше персонажа), suspense (зритель знает столько же и боится исхода), mystery (зритель знает меньше) и surprise (неожиданная информация) [2]. Dramatic irony — самый универсальный инструмент: он генерирует конфликт, эмоции, комедию и напряжение, не меняя ни сюжет, ни персонажей [3]. Секреты — один из мощнейших драматических инструментов: они создают напряжение, саспенс, иронию и постоянную угрозу разоблачения [4]. При этом информацию нужно не только удерживать, но и своевременно раскрывать — затягивание откровений за пределы первого сезона рискует потерять терпение аудитории [5].

Цитаты

[1] "I call it the M-Factor: the way information is managed (or mismanaged) in a story. [...] All story problems can be traced back to issues with structure, story-type or M-Factor." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Section 2.4, p.111

[2] "Dramatic irony is when the audience knows more than at least one character. Suspense is when the audience knows as much as the protagonist and fears what might happen. Mystery is when the audience knows less than at least one character. Surprise is when the audience gets unexpected information." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Section 2.4, p.112

[3] "Dramatic irony is arguably the most versatile and powerful tool to generate conflict, emotion, identification, interest, comedy, suspense, tension, irony — you name it — without changing anything in the plot or the characters. All you're changing is who knows what and when." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Section 2.4, p.113

[4] "Secrets are one of the most powerful dramatic tools available to writers. How can we inject some kind of secret into this story and amp everything up? Secrets create tension, suspense, irony and the constant threat of exposure." — Levy, p.157

[5] "You can always create another central mystery in subsequent seasons, but suspending revelation for too long – beyond season 1 – is perhaps testing your patient audience's good will." — Landau, p.269

[6] "Managing information is the third key dimension of screenwriting, along with structure and story-type. It defines who knows what and when in the story: the writer, the characters, and the audience." — Oberg, Quick Overview, p.18

[7] "Consider the balance between mystery and suspense in your series. A mystery-heavy series keeps the audience guessing, while a suspense-heavy series keeps them on the edge of their seat. Both are powerful, but too much mystery can be frustrating and too much suspense can be exhausting." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.1, p.173

[8] "Very often, if a pilot feels flat, predictable or linear, it's due to weak management of information." — Oberg, Chapter 3, Section 3.2, p.210

[9] "A closed mystery is one in which the main question is answered by the end of the episode or the season. An open mystery is one in which the main question spans the whole season or even the whole series." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Section 2.4, p.118

[10] "Sequencing is the order in which we tell the story. It's about deciding when to reveal information to the audience. Weaving is how we alternate between storylines/strands within an episode. Both are key tools for managing information and pacing." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Case Study: Stranger Things, p.140

[11] "The M-Factor (management of information) in Stranger Things S1 is crucial: there is dramatic irony between strands (the audience knows Will is in the Upside Down before the characters do), mystery (what is the Upside Down?), suspense (will they find Will in time?) and surprise (Eleven's powers, Hopper's deal with Brenner)." — Oberg, Chapter 2, Case Study: Stranger Things, p.132

[12] "Remember, when plotting, it's not only Who Does What When, but also Who Knows What When, along with each character's immediate or delayed reaction." — Landau, p.260

[13] "Information is power. It's not just about who does what, when, but who knows what, when." — Landau, p.184

[14] "Suspense has two components: Anticipation (of what's going to happen next) and Surprise (when it actually happens, hopefully in an unexpected, inevitable but not predictable way)." — Landau, p.261

[15] "A good rule of thumb is that a strong, viable cliffhanger should grow out of character jeopardy, risk, or fear. A bad cliffhanger is just a plot point that comes out of nowhere solely for shock value." — Landau, p.268

[16] "So even a 'surprise' cliffhanger needs to be (subtly) set up (or foreshadowed) so that the audience feels they should have seen it coming – but didn't." — Landau, p.268

Связи

  • Актовая структура эпизода — act breaks часто строятся на управлении информацией (cliffhangers, revelations)
  • Пилотный эпизод — плоский пилот почти всегда страдает от слабого M-Factor
  • Конструкция сцены — в каждой сцене определяй: кто знает что в этот момент
  • Диалог и подтекст — subtext работает через разрыв между тем, что персонаж знает и что говорит
